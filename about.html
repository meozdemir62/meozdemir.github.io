education

I started my undergraduate studies at the University of California, Berkeley, in 2019. In my second year, having always had a particular interest in cancer and wanting to learn more about it, I declared a concentration in Immunology and started learning about immune systems, cancer, and how tumors interact with/shape the immune microenvironments surrounding them.

Though my formal education was heavily biology-focused, in my last semester of college, I took a comprehensive Introduction to Computer Science and Programming course and immediately fell in love with it. Having had a taste of what computer science has to offer, I finished college feeling a little sad about my relatively late introduction to the field, yet with the knowledge that it wasn't too late, and curious to learn more.

undergraduate research

In my second year of college, I joined the lab of Michel DuPage, where I embarked on my cancer research journey. In the DuPage Lab I worked at the intersection of cancer biology, immunology and epigenetics, to explore ways to selectively reprogram regulatory T cells to enhance anti-tumor immunity while eliminating risk for autoimmune toxicity. I wrote and defended my undergraduate honors thesis about the role of EN101, a cysteine-reactive compound, on Foxp3 regulation in Tregs.

work

In 2023, I graduated from UC Berkeley with a degree in Molecular & Cell Biology (with a concentration of Immunology) and moved to Boston to start working at the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute. Here, I joined the lab of Birgit Knoechel, and was introduced to the exciting world of cutting edge single cell sequencing technologies and bioinformatics.

Now, I have a lot of experience in single cell RNA-sequencing, particularly in Chromium Single Cell Gene Expression, Cell Surface Protein Expression and TCR/BCR analysis via 10X genomics. In addition to scRNA-seq, I also currently work on CAR T-cell therapies with a special focus on preventing resistance.
